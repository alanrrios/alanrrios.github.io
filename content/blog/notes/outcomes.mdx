# Manipulated Outcomes - Surveillance Led Influence

Often I'll find myself reflecting on my position. How is it that someone so ambitious ends up so impoverished? 

That even to this day, as I build completely [innovative solutions](/budgiter/) with entirely [new tech](/projects/pursuits/), I'm still using mobile app to pick up general labor side gigs.

## Established Sytems

There's a system that I had to learn early on in my *career*, if one could call it that, that revolved around optimizing for.. caloric intake. It's not that I was on a diet; it's that I could not afford to feed to myself. I went from being a competitive swimmer on an excessive caloric diet to maintain the training regime that demanded to scavenging for food and discounts. 

That system was [Calories by the Dollar](/notes/poverty): how many calories I was able to get for each dollar I spent. I experimented with different discounts and coupons to divide the total calories by the total cost of the meal.

## Sufficient Opportunity 

```
On paper, the math of my career should have yielded a different equation. I possessed a unique multiplier: foundational expertise in sales and customer service paired with full-stack web development and server management. When you cross-examine that commercial and technical capability with a dozen distinct product ideas, the expected output is a breakthrough. Instead, the result was a strange, stagnant drought.

This timeline wasn’t a failure of focus, but a testament to broad adaptability. Seamlessly pivoting from air health to blockchain gaming proved a repeatable capacity to isolate and design solutions around wildly different problem sets. I had the tools to bring abstract concepts into tangible realities, yet despite a decade of building and a cross-functional toolset, the ledger remains blank. I am left confronting an ecosystem that offered no professional avenues or startup runways to actually employ them—leaving all that raw materials with nowhere to go.
```

## Costs of Admission

```
It is easy to romanticize the journey of a self-taught builder, but the reality is dictated by a harsh financial gravity. Navigating these pursuits from a low-income baseline meant that every hour spent coding was an hour borrowed from basic survival. In the software world, code might be free, but the capital required to actually scale, secure, and launch a viable startup is a luxury. While a Silicon Valley archetype can rely on a safety net or a family seed round to bridge the gap between prototype and production, my journey was a constant compromise between the audacity to build and the necessity to eat.

Ultimately, this forced a profound shift in how I view the destination. The traditional metrics of startup success—venture backing, monetization, market capture—were locked behind a financial gate I couldn't yet pay to pass. But if the destination remained out of reach, the journey itself became a forced evolution. I didn’t get a capitalized enterprise, but I built a dangerous, cross-functional skill set out of absolute necessity. The outcome wasn’t a company; it was a craftsman who now knows exactly how to build the machine from scratch.
```
